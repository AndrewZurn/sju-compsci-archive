In the project, you are to redo the cannon calculations from Lab 5 in a different way. You will compute the position vertically and horizontally after very small time intervals assuming the velocity stays constant over that interval. You will then adjust the vertical velocity (ONLY) based on the gravitational force and compute over the next small time interval with this new velocity. Repeat this computation until the cannonball hits the ground.




Recall this diagram from Lab 5. If we start out with an initial velocity v0 at angle A, then we get the vertical velocity and horizontal velocity as shown. Thus, at the beginning of our iterative process, the vertical and horizontal velocity will be as shown in the figure above. If we do the same assumptions as last time, then the change in vertical velocity over the next very small time interval dt will be given as -g*dt where g is the acceleration of gravity and there will be no change in horizontal velocity. Thus after time dt, the vertical velocity will decrease by g*dt. In programmatic form, we can do the following:


//Initial values for verticalVelocity and horizontalVelocity.
verticalVelocity=v0*Math.sin(A);
horizontalVelocity=v0*Math.cos(A);

//Initial ball position

yPosition=canvas.getHeight()-BALLSIZE;

//Time elapsed so far.
time=0.0;

//Time interval to recheck new xPosition and yPosition. Better to have this as a constant called DT
dt=0.01;

//while the ball is above the lower edge of the canvas…i.e. in the air
while(yPosition <= canvas.getHeight()-BALLSIZE) {
       //Distance to move in the x and y directions after dt seconds. dx increases while dy decreases since he ball moves upwards to the right
   dx = horizontalVelocity*dt;
   dy = -verticalVelocity*dt;
        //First we calculate the horizontal and vertical positions after dt seconds.
        //We assume the velocities remain approximately constant over that time.
   xPosition += dx;
   yPosition += dy;
        //Move ball by dx in the x-direction (i.e. right) and dy in the y-direction (i.e. up) and pause for a while
        ????
        //Then we recompute the vertical velocity under the same assumptions
   verticalVelocity -= g*dt;
}

When we exit the while loop, the horizontal position will be where the cannonball hit the ground.

Your task is to revise the program to have a cannonball actually move across the canvas according to the calculations. Since you know the horizontal and vertical velocities, you can move the cannonball appropriately (think about how far the cannonball should move in the x and y position in time dt ... call the movements values dx and dy (recall that distance = velocity*time)).  When the vertical position is back at canvas.getHeight()-BALLSIZE (i.e. the ball has hit the ground again), your ball should reach the ground and you should halt.
